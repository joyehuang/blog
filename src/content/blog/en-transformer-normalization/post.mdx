> This is the English edition of the first post in my MiniMind study series.

## Why This Matters

If you train or analyze deep Transformer models, normalization stops being a detail very quickly. Without it, optimization becomes unstable, gradients become harder to control, and deeper stacks become much more difficult to train.

That is why normalization layers are not optional decoration in large language models. They are part of the mechanism that keeps the model trainable.

## The Core Problem

As networks become deeper, activations and gradients can drift. Once that drift accumulates across dozens of layers, optimization quality drops sharply:

- gradients can explode or vanish,
- training becomes more sensitive to initialization,
- and later layers become harder to update reliably.

Transformers are especially exposed to this because the residual pathway keeps stacking transformations on top of previous states.

## What Normalization Actually Does

Normalization stabilizes the scale of the hidden states moving through the network. In practice, that means:

- smoother optimization,
- better behavior under depth,
- more predictable residual updates,
- and less sensitivity to extreme activation values.

The exact math differs across LayerNorm, RMSNorm, and other variants, but the engineering goal is similar: make each block easier to optimize.

## Why RMSNorm Became Popular

RMSNorm removes the mean-centering step from LayerNorm and keeps only the root-mean-square scaling. That makes it simpler and slightly cheaper while still preserving the stability benefits that matter in practice.

For modern LLMs, this tradeoff is attractive:

- it is lighter than LayerNorm,
- it works well at scale,
- and it fits naturally into pre-norm Transformer architectures.

That is why you see RMSNorm appear repeatedly in recent open-weight and production-grade language models.

## A Good Mental Model

I think about normalization as a control layer on the signal flowing through the stack. Attention and feed-forward layers decide how to transform information. Normalization keeps those transformations from becoming numerically unruly as depth increases.

It does not make the model smart by itself. It makes the rest of the model trainable enough to become smart.
